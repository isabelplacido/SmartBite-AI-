SmartBite AI

Food Awareness & Chronic Disease Prevention with Deep Learning

Problem & Motivation Technical Approach Results & Takeaways

The Problem

Chronic diseases like diabetes, heart disease, and hypertension are tightly linked to what people eat every day. A lot of products that look harmless on the shelf are actually ultra-processed and loaded with added sugar, salt, and unhealthy fats. Nutrition labels are confusing and time-consuming to read, so most people just guess and move on.

These small, fast decisions in the grocery aisle, at vending machines, or on delivery apps slowly add up and increase long-term health risk, especially for young adults who are just building their habits.

Motivation

Expecting everyone to count calories, track macros, and analyze ingredients for every meal is unrealistic. What would help more is a simple signal about food quality that fits into the way people already behave: scrolling, snapping pictures, and tapping once for feedback.

The idea behind SmartBite AI is to explore whether computer vision can provide that signal. Instead of manually logging foods, a user could take a quick photo of a product and get a coarse judgment of how health-supporting it is.

Project Idea

This project builds a small image classifier that groups food items into three broad categories:

  • Fresh – foods that are closer to whole or minimally processed items.
  • Mixed – foods that combine fresh and processed elements or are more neutral.
  • Ultraprocessed – highly processed snacks or products with many added ingredients.

The goal is not to give medical advice or exact nutrition scores. Instead, this is a proof-of-concept: can a deep learning model like AlexNet (and a more modern ResNet18) learn useful visual patterns from a small custom dataset and separate these three categories at all?

Goals of This Project

  • Prototype an image-based “food awareness” tool with three health-related classes.
  • Adapt a pretrained AlexNet model to a new dataset of food images.
  • Run controlled experiments with batch size, learning rate, augmentation, and architecture.
  • Evaluate performance limits on a small dataset and reflect on what would be needed in real life.
